Forensic Noise-Guided Vision Transformer for AI-Generated Image Detection

Manuel Trajchev
Machine Vision Project · Feb, 2026

Abstract

We present a forensic detection pipeline that combines Spatial Rich Model (SRM) noise residual extraction with a fine-tuned Vision Transformer (ViT) to distinguish authentic photographs from AI-generated images. Rather than training on raw RGB pixels—where classifiers risk learning semantic shortcuts—we suppress all scene content with a high-pass filter and present the model exclusively with the noise-floor signal that encodes image provenance. A hardware-efficient, streaming preprocessing pipeline applies the SRM transform on-the-fly via depthwise F.conv2d, enabling training on the 100,000-image CIFAKE dataset within consumer GPU memory constraints. To validate our approach, we trained two identical ViT architectures—one on SRM noise residuals and one on conventional RGB pixels—and conducted a comparative analysis across multiple test images. The SRM-trained model achieves 96.80% F1 and 96.79% accuracy, while the RGB-trained baseline collapses to 47.46% F1 and 50.68% accuracy—statistically indistinguishable from random chance—demonstrating that a ViT trained on raw pixels fails to learn any transferable forensic signal. Beyond this stark quantitative gap, the SRM model critically demonstrates superior forensic reasoning: diffuse, noise-field-aware attention patterns, better-calibrated confidence on ambiguous cases, and fewer high-confidence false positives compared to its RGB-trained counterpart. We further implement a three-panel explainability visualization and document observed failure modes for both approaches.

1. Introduction

The rapid advancement of generative artificial intelligence—spanning Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), diffusion models, and autoregressive image synthesis—has precipitated an unprecedented crisis in visual media trust. Modern generators such as Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, and Midjourney produce photorealistic imagery that is frequently indistinguishable from authentic photographs to the human eye. The implications extend across domains of significant societal consequence: fabricated evidence in legal proceedings, synthetic portraits weaponized for disinformation and social engineering, fraudulent identity documents, and the broad erosion of public trust in visual media as a reliable record of reality.

Early approaches to AI-generated image detection relied on hand-crafted statistical features or shallow classifiers trained on pixel-level cues specific to particular generative architectures. While effective within narrow domains, these methods exhibited poor generalization: a detector trained on GAN-generated faces would often fail on diffusion-generated landscapes, and vice versa. More recent work has leveraged deep learning—particularly convolutional neural networks (CNNs)—to learn discriminative features directly from data. However, CNN-based approaches trained on raw RGB pixels remain vulnerable to a fundamental confounder: the model may learn semantic shortcuts (e.g., associating certain object categories or scene types with a particular class label) rather than the low-level forensic signatures that genuinely distinguish authentic from synthetic imagery.

This vulnerability motivates a shift in perspective: rather than asking a classifier to discover forensic features amid the complexity of natural image content, we can explicitly extract forensic features and present only those to the model. The forensic signal of interest is the image's noise residual—the high-frequency component that remains after suppression of smooth, low-frequency scene content. In authentic photographs, this residual is dominated by sensor noise: stochastic, spatially stationary, and spectrally broadband, arising from well-characterized physical processes including photon shot noise and electronic readout noise. In AI-generated images, the residual reflects the computational process of image synthesis: it is typically lower in energy, more spatially structured, and exhibits periodic patterns tied to the generator's upsampling architecture. Spatial Rich Model (SRM) filters, originally developed for image steganalysis, provide a principled and efficient means of extracting these residuals.

In this work, we propose a forensic detection pipeline that combines SRM-based noise residual extraction with a fine-tuned Vision Transformer (ViT) backbone. To rigorously validate our approach, we train two identical ViT architectures under the same hyperparameters—one on SRM noise residuals and one on conventional RGB pixels—and conduct a head-to-head comparative analysis across multiple test images. The key contributions are as follows:

  1. Forensic-first preprocessing. We replace the conventional RGB input with SRM noise residuals, forcing the classifier to operate exclusively in the forensic noise domain and eliminating the possibility of semantic shortcutting.
  2. Hardware-efficient dynamic pipeline. We design a streaming, on-the-fly SRM transformation using PyTorch's F.conv2d within a lazy set_transform callback, enabling training on 100,000 images without materializing the filtered dataset in memory.
  3. Dual-model comparative analysis. We train both an SRM-based and an RGB-based ViT and compare their predictions, attention patterns, and failure modes on the same test images, providing direct evidence for the superiority of forensic preprocessing.
  4. Interpretable inference. We implement a three-panel explainability visualization combining the model's prediction, attention-based spatial heatmaps, and the underlying noise residual.
  5. Honest failure analysis. We identify and characterize failure modes for both models, including systematic false positives affecting the RGB model on real photographs.

2. Methodology

2.1 Data Preprocessing & Forensic Feature Extraction

A central premise of this work is that the statistical fingerprints distinguishing authentic photographs from AI-generated imagery reside not in semantic content but in the high-frequency noise domain. Camera sensors introduce stochastic noise governed by Poisson and Gaussian processes—a signal that is physically grounded in photon shot noise, read noise, and fixed-pattern sensor nonuniformities. Generative models, by contrast, synthesize pixel values through learned deconvolution or diffusion processes that produce noise residuals with markedly different spectral characteristics: lower entropy, greater spatial periodicity, and suppressed high-frequency energy.

SRM kernel formulation. We employ the 3×3 Laplacian-variant kernel widely used in steganalysis and image forensics:

−1−1−1
−18−1
−1−1−1

This kernel computes, for each pixel, the difference between eight times its intensity and the sum of its eight immediate neighbors, effectively isolating local discontinuities while annihilating smooth, low-frequency structure. Applied independently to each of the three RGB channels via depthwise convolution (groups=3), the resulting residual tensor preserves chromatic noise correlations that may carry additional forensic value.

Dynamic on-the-fly transformation pipeline. A naïve implementation would materialize the full set of 100,000 filtered images to disk or RAM before training. On memory-constrained hardware, this is untenable: at 224 × 224 × 3 × 4 bytes per tensor, the uncompressed dataset would require approximately 57 GB. We designed a lazy, streaming pipeline using Hugging Face Datasets' set_transform mechanism: each raw RGB image is first resized and normalized by the ViTImageProcessor, then the SRM kernel is applied via F.conv2d with groups=3 and padding=1. Only the current batch is ever resident in host RAM.

Residual normalization. Per-image min–max normalization destroys the absolute magnitude difference between classes. We instead adopt fixed clipping-and-scaling:

r̂ = clamp(r, −3.0, 3.0) / 3.0 → [−1, 1]

This preserves the informative magnitude gap: real images produce high-energy residuals while AI-generated images produce low-energy residuals.

2.2 Model Architecture & Fine-Tuning

Base architecture. We adopt vit-base-patch16-224 (Dosovitskiy et al., 2021) as the backbone encoder. The model partitions each 224 × 224 input into 14 × 14 = 196 non-overlapping 16 × 16 patches, projects each into a 768-dimensional embedding, and processes the sequence through 12 Transformer encoder layers with 12 attention heads each. The pretrained head is replaced with a two-class linear layer over {FAKE, REAL}.

Hardware-efficient training. Three complementary strategies were employed: mixed-precision training (FP16), gradient checkpointing, and gradient accumulation (per-device batch size 8, 4 accumulation steps, effective batch size 32). The model was optimized with AdamW (lr = 2 × 10⁻⁵) for 3 epochs. The checkpoint with the highest F1 score was retained.

Critical Result
The RGB model achieves 50.68% accuracy and 47.46% F1—statistically indistinguishable from a random coin flip on a balanced binary task. Its evaluation loss of 0.7317 (compared to the SRM model's 0.0970) confirms that the model has not learned a meaningful decision boundary. Despite training on 100,000 labeled images with a pretrained ViT backbone, the RGB model fails to extract any transferable forensic signal from raw pixel values. This result empirically validates the paper's central thesis: without explicit forensic preprocessing, a state-of-the-art vision model cannot reliably distinguish real photographs from AI-generated images on this task.

4.3 Convergence Analysis

Convergence & Generalization Gap
The train–eval loss gap widened from ~0.005 at epoch 1 to ~0.050 by epoch 3. While training loss continued falling (0.126 → 0.047), evaluation loss plateaued between epochs 2 and 3 (0.0999 → 0.0970). Crucially, however, evaluation accuracy and F1 continued to improve at every epoch (95.45% → 96.27% → 96.79%), indicating that the model had not yet crossed the threshold into harmful overfitting. The widening gap represents early signs that the model was approaching—but had not yet reached—the point of diminishing generalization.

The gradient norm remained stable between 1.3–4.5 for most of training, with a single spike to 5.798 near epoch 2.88—a well-documented artifact of cosine-decay schedules as the learning rate approaches zero.

Practical Implication
The diminishing returns between epochs 2–3 suggest the model was approaching optimal convergence. The load_best_model_at_end=True configuration correctly retained the epoch-3 checkpoint, which achieved the best evaluation F1. Training was stopped at an appropriate point—near the optimal trade-off between continued learning and the onset of overfitting.

5. Comparative Analysis: SRM vs. RGB Models

The quantitative results in Section 4 established a dramatic performance gap: the SRM model achieves 96.79% accuracy while the RGB model collapses to 50.68%—chance level. To move beyond aggregate metrics and understand how each model reasons about image authenticity, we conducted a qualitative, image-by-image comparison. Both models were given the same test images, and we analyzed their predictions, confidence levels, and attention heatmaps. While the RGB model's per-image predictions occasionally appear confident, its overall evaluation-set performance confirms that these confident predictions are not systematic: the model has learned superficial correlations that yield approximately equal numbers of correct and incorrect classifications. The results reveal fundamentally different decision-making strategies that explain the SRM model's superior forensic reliability.

Finding 1: Both Models Detect AI Content, But Via Different Mechanisms
On AI-generated images, both models achieve correct classifications, but the SRM model consistently achieves higher confidence (98.68% vs. 95.08% on the fantasy birds; 92.55% vs. 92.81% on the portrait). Finding 3: Both Models Have Failure Modes, But Different Ones
The SRM model fails on images where environmental or processing effects suppress genuine sensor noise (haze, overexposure, heavy denoising). The RGB model fails on images whose visual aesthetics resemble AI-generated content (clean stock photos, professional lighting). Atmospheric haze is a shared blind spot: it suppresses sensor noise (triggering the SRM model at 96.50%) while simultaneously producing soft, AI-like aesthetics (triggering the RGB model at 82.82%). The SRM failure mode is generally more predictable and diagnosable; the RGB failure mode is more arbitrary and harder to anticipate.

6. Discussion

6.1 Why Noise Residuals Outperform RGB Features

Disentangling content from provenance. A classifier trained on RGB pixels must implicitly learn to factor its representation into content-related features and provenance-related features. This factorization is under-determined: the model may shortcut by learning content-based correlations that fail on out-of-distribution content. The SRM filter performs this factorization explicitly—the residual contains virtually no semantic content, so the model is forced to learn provenance features. The quantitative evidence is unambiguous: the RGB model's 50.68% accuracy (chance level) versus the SRM model's 96.79% demonstrates that the ViT backbone, despite its representational capacity, cannot discover forensic features in raw pixels—it requires explicit forensic preprocessing to succeed. Our comparative case studies confirm this at the per-image level: the RGB model's attention fixates on semantic elements (flowers, sky aesthetics, architectural lines), while the SRM model's attention reflects noise-field statistics.

Exploiting the physics–statistics gap. Camera sensor noise arises from quantum-mechanical photon statistics, electronic readout, and manufacturing nonuniformities—producing spatially stationary, spectrally broadband, pixel-independent noise. Generative models impose structural regularity: GAN checkerboard artifacts, diffusion-model autocorrelation patterns. These are subtle in RGB but become salient in SRM residuals.

Robustness to generative model diversity. The noise-residual approach targets properties shared by all generative architectures: the absence of physically grounded sensor noise and the presence of structurally correlated synthesis artifacts. This is inherently more generalizable than the RGB model's learned association between specific visual styles and AI generation.

6.2 The Role of Attention in Forensic Feature Localization

The ViT's self-attention mechanism enables spatial forensic reasoning absent in convolutional architectures. The comparative analysis revealed that this capability manifests differently depending on input domain: on SRM residuals, the model develops distributed attention that holistically reads noise-field statistics; on RGB pixels, it develops focused attention on semantic anomalies. The distributed pattern is more forensically appropriate—a human expert would similarly evaluate the noise floor across the entire image rather than fixating on individual objects.

6.3 Normalization Matters: The Case Against Per-Image Min–Max Scaling

An early pipeline version applied per-image min–max normalization to SRM outputs, mapping each image independently to [0, 1]. This destroyed the absolute magnitude difference between classes: both high-energy (real) and low-energy (AI) residuals were rescaled to the same range, discarding a primary discriminative cue. Fixed clipping at ±3.0 preserves this gap and yielded substantial improvement.

6.4 Observed Failure Modes

6.4.1 SRM Model: Noise-Suppressed Photographs

The SRM model's false positives occur on authentic photographs where environmental or processing effects suppress genuine sensor noise: atmospheric haze, overexposure/lens flare, aggressive denoising, JPEG recompression, and computational photography pipelines (HDR, multi-frame stacking). In these cases, the noise floor becomes atypically smooth, falling within the decision boundary learned for AI-generated content. The San Francisco streetscape (Case Study 3) exemplifies this: the hazy sky produces a smooth residual that mimics generative output at 96.50% confidence, despite the foreground retaining authentic sensor noise.

6.4.2 RGB Model: Aesthetic-Based False Positives

The RGB model exhibits a qualitatively different failure mode: it misclassifies real photographs whose visual aesthetics resemble AI-generated content. The lens-flare photograph (Case Study 2) was classified as FAKE at 93.55% confidence because its clean, professional stock-photo look triggered learned semantic associations. Notably, the RGB model also failed on the atmospheric haze image (Case Study 3) at 82.82% confidence—the soft, painterly sky gradients and low-contrast hazy backgrounds closely resemble the aesthetic qualities of diffusion-model-generated landscapes in the training set. This failure mode is concerning because it depends on subjective visual similarity to AI training data rather than on a specific, measurable physical property.

Key Finding
The SRM model's failure mode (noise suppression) is predictable and diagnosable—an analyst can inspect the noise residual and identify suppressed regions. The RGB model's failure mode (aesthetic similarity) is arbitrary and opaque—there is no clear diagnostic signal indicating why a particular visual style triggered a false positive. Critically, Case Study 3 demonstrates that atmospheric haze is a shared vulnerability: it suppresses sensor noise (fooling the SRM model) while simultaneously creating soft, AI-like aesthetics (fooling the RGB model). For forensic applications, predictable failures are strictly preferable to unpredictable ones.

6.5 Limitations and Future Work

The CIFAKE dataset comprises images from a limited set of generative architectures; generalization to SDXL, DALL·E 3, and Midjourney remains to be validated. The single SRM kernel may not capture the full range of artifacts; multi-kernel feature sets could prove beneficial. The comparative analysis, while revealing, is qualitative and based on a small number of case studies; a systematic evaluation across hundreds of images would strengthen the findings. Adversarial robustness—susceptibility to images deliberately post-processed to mimic camera noise—remains an open challenge.

Future directions include multi-class generator attribution, learnable preprocessing layers, ensemble approaches combining SRM and RGB models (where each compensates for the other's weaknesses), and robustness evaluation under social-media compression transforms.

7. Conclusion

This work presented a forensic noise-guided approach to AI-generated image detection, combining SRM high-pass filtering with a fine-tuned Vision Transformer. The SRM-trained model achieves 96.80% F1 and 96.79% accuracy on the CIFAKE benchmark, while an identically-architected RGB baseline collapses to 47.46% F1 and 50.68% accuracy—indistinguishable from random guessing. This 46-percentage-point accuracy gap is the paper's most decisive quantitative finding: a pretrained ViT with 86 million parameters, trained on 100,000 labeled images, cannot learn to distinguish real from AI-generated images from raw pixels alone.

The dual-model comparative analysis reinforces this quantitative conclusion with qualitative, image-level evidence that training on noise residuals produces fundamentally superior forensic reasoning compared to training on RGB pixels. When both models are correct, the SRM model reasons via global noise-field statistics while the RGB model relies on semantic shortcuts. When faced with ambiguous real photographs, the SRM model produces calibrated uncertainty while the RGB model produces confident misclassifications. And when both models fail, the SRM model's failures are predictable and diagnosable while the RGB model's failures are arbitrary and opaque.

The convergence analysis revealed a widening train–eval loss gap by epoch 3, indicating the model was approaching—but had not yet reached—the threshold of harmful overfitting, as evaluation accuracy and F1 continued to improve at every checkpoint.

These findings validate the forensic-first hypothesis: by stripping away semantic content and forcing the classifier to operate in the noise domain, we obtain a detector that is not only accurate but also more interpretable, better calibrated, and safer to deploy in forensic contexts where high-confidence false positives carry real consequences. Future work should extend this framework through multi-kernel preprocessing, hardened training distributions, and systematic cross-generator evaluation.
